Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the sun in our solar system. It's known as the 'heater' because of its extremely high temperature that can reach up to 430 degrees Celsius during the day and drop to minus 180 degrees Celsius at night. Mercury is also the smallest planet in our solar system, with a diameter only a quarter of Earth's. It has a very slow rotation, so one day on Mercury is equivalent to two orbits around the sun.

Venus

Venus is often called Earth's 'sister planet' due to its similar size and composition. However, Venus has a very different atmosphere, consisting mostly of carbon dioxide and clouds of sulfuric acid that trap heat, making it the hottest planet in our solar system with surface temperatures that can reach up to 471 degrees Celsius. Venus also rotates slowly in the opposite direction to most planets, meaning that its day is longer than its year. Despite its proximity to Earth, Venus remains one of the most mysterious planets in our solar system, and there is still much to be discovered about this fascinating planet.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the sun and the only known planet that supports life. It's often called the 'Blue Planet' because of its vast oceans that cover about 71% of its surface. Earth is the largest of the terrestrial planets and has a diverse range of climates and ecosystems, from freezing polar regions to scorching deserts, lush rainforests to vast oceans. The planet's atmosphere is also unique, containing the right mix of gases that allow for life to thrive. Earth orbits the sun at just the right distance to maintain liquid water on its surface, making it an ideal planet for sustaining life as we know it.

Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the sun, known for its reddish appearance caused by iron oxide on its surface. It has a thin atmosphere mostly made of carbon dioxide and a dusty, rocky landscape. Scientists believe it may have had liquid water and potential for life. Mars is a target for future human exploration and colonization.
